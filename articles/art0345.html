    Sleep and Dreams
by   The Connection

Deirdre Barrett, psychologist, and author of "The Committee of Sleep: How Artists, Scientists and Athletes Use Dreams for Creative Problem-Solving - And How You Can Too" and Ray Kurzweil, an inventor who has found inspiration for his practical ideas in his dreams, discuss sleep and dreaming on NPR's The Connection.


Originally aired March 29, 2001 at The Connection. Published on KurzweilAI.net November 6, 2001.

"To sleep, perchance to dream." Shakespeare had it right. If we allow them, dreams dance in our subconscious as a subtle source of inspiration of art and invention.

Paul McCartney, Stephen King, Stravinsky and Jasper Johns, all pulled on this internal muse. From Stravinsky's Rite of Spring to Mary Shelley's Frankenstein, dreams have come alive as symphonies, books and theatrical productions. John Steinbeck coined the phrase "Sleep on It!" based on his belief that some mysterious force worked while we slumber. Scientists and athletes also have drawn from the well of dreams. Marion Jones dreamed of running the "perfect race" long before she won five Olympic medals.

A dream inspired Gandhi to call on his countrymen to engage in a hunger strike to protest British rule. But not all of us enjoy a lively dream life. We're simply dragging along like mummies, deprived of the pleasure of dreaming. But it doesn't have to be this way...
